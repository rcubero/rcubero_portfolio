Mammalian hearts lose their ability to proliferate shortly after birth. When the heart gets damaged, the functioning heart cells, called cardiomyocytes, attempt to proliferate however, their capacity to replace the injured areas is highly limited that connective tissues fill in the large gaps. This results in a heart that's barely beating until the next inevitable cardiomyopathic injury. Understanding the mechanisms that reactivate cardiomyocyte proliferation is key to address cardiovascular diseases.
In 2012, a group of molecular biologists from Dr. Mauro Giacca's laboratory in the International Center for Genetic Engineering and Biotechnology, led by Dr. Ana Eulalio, discovered that certain microRNAs that were exogenously injected into rat hearts damaged by incision promoted cardiomyocyte proliferation and closed the injury. Two of these identified highly pro-proliferative microRNAs were hsa-miR-590-3p and hsa-miR-199a-3p. MicroRNAs are short RNA sequences that inhibit the translation of mRNAs that they bind to, thus exogenously transfecting cardiomyocytes with these pro-proliferative microRNAs would generally downregulate the protein expression of their target mRNAs which cascades down the interaction network and eventually cause certain transcription factors to activate and complete the cell cycle. Indeed, when the group performed bulk RNA sequencing of cardiomyocytes transfected with either miR-590-3p or miR-199a-3p, they observed upregulation of mRNAs involved in cell cycle and downregulation in connective tissue signaling.
