During my postdoctoral fellowship, I looked into a highly dynamic non-neuron cell type called microglia that colonize the central nervous system (CNS) during the embryonic stage and perform multiplexed functions like synapse refinement, debris clearing and injury response throughout the organisms lifetime.
As these tiny cells have to change their shapes to squeeze through crevices made up of neuronal connections, extracellular matrix and other cell type bodies, we hypothesized that microglial morphologies carry information about the state of its immediate environment.
As these tiny cells have to change their shapes to squeeze through crevices made up of neuronal connections, extracellular matrix and other cell type bodies, we hypothesized that microglial morphologies carry information about the state of its immediate environment.
Indeed, by developing MorphOMICs that combines topology, manifold learning and statistical techniques to analyze 3D microglia morphology reconstructions from fluorescence microscopy images, I have unraveled region- and sex-specific morphological signatures (Colombo and Cubero et al., 2022) and arranged the morphological adaptations as an atlas which recaps developmental and degeneration timelines (Fig 2A).
This approach allowed me to robustly capture subtle morphological differences and identify layer-specific responses to external perturbation, e.g., exposure and recovery to anesthetic doses of ketamine (Fig 2B).
When we complemented with the analyses of single nuclei transcriptomes and open chromatin accessibility of cell populations in the visual cortex, and of the in vivo electrophysiological recordings of surrounding neurons, we found layer-specific differences in microglial gene expression patterns, and confirmed cell-cell interactions between microglia and neuronal populations (Venturino et al., in preparation).
